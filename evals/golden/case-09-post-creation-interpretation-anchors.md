# Golden anchors — Case 09: post-creation interpretation

## What a strong answer must do

### Correct object identification
- Identify the target as a **workflow-discipline skill** — its value comes from process constraints (blame-free framing, iterative root cause analysis, mandatory action items), not from automation or clever generation.
- Recognize the collaboration context: this skill was built with skill-creator, refined through skills-refiner audit, and now needs an explanation for the team that will use it.

### Article quality
- Produce a team-readable explanation, not an audit report or scorecard.
- The output should work as onboarding material: a team member reading it should understand what the skill does, why it is designed this way, and what to expect when using it.
- Lead with a clear thesis about the skill's core value proposition.

### Mechanism explanation
A strong answer should explain at least 2 of the following:

- Why refusing "human error" as a root cause forces deeper systemic analysis: it prevents the investigation from stopping at the most visible failure point and pushes toward the conditions that made the error possible.
- Why requiring every finding to connect to an action item prevents postmortems from becoming empty exercises: it forces the team to commit to change rather than merely documenting what happened.
- Why the blame-free constraint is structural, not just cultural: by embedding it in the skill's instructions rather than relying on team norms, it becomes the default behavior regardless of who runs the postmortem.
- Why the iterative "why" technique matters: shallow root cause analysis produces surface fixes that do not prevent recurrence.

### Real limits to surface
A strong answer should surface at least 1 of the following:

- The skill does not handle recurring incidents that trace back to the same root cause across multiple postmortems — there is no cross-incident pattern recognition.
- There is no guidance on prioritizing action items when the list is long — all items appear equal in urgency.
- The skill does not address follow-up tracking: action items are listed but there is no mechanism to verify they were completed.
- The iterative "why" technique can stall if participants do not have enough systemic knowledge to go deeper.
- The skill assumes a single incident with a traceable timeline — complex incidents involving multiple interacting failures may not fit the linear timeline model.

### Transfer value
A strong answer should surface at least 1 transferable lesson, such as:

- Embedding process discipline as skill constraints (rather than relying on human norms) is a pattern that works beyond postmortems.
- The "connect every finding to an action" pattern prevents analysis-heavy, action-light outputs in any skill that produces recommendations.
- Structural blame-avoidance (refusing a category of answer) is a design technique that can shape behavior more reliably than a general tone instruction.

---

## Failure signals

Treat the answer as failing if it:
- Produces a scorecard or structured audit report instead of a readable explanation
- Describes the skill's features without explaining why the design choices matter
- Writes for skill designers rather than for the team that will use the skill
- Uses hollow praise ("remarkably well-designed", "truly innovative") without grounding
- Sounds obviously machine-generated: padded opening, templated transitions, forced symmetry
- Ignores the collaboration context (that this was built with skill-creator and audited by skills-refiner)

## Pass conditions

- Target identification and purpose fit: ≥ 4
- Mechanism explanation: ≥ 4
- Writing quality: ≥ 4
- Low "AI flavor": ≥ 4
- Overall average: ≥ 4.0
